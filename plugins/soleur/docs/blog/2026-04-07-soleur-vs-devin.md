[Devin](https://cognition.ai/) is the most capable autonomous software engineer available today. It ships code, writes tests, fixes bugs, reads documentation, and opens pull requests — without a human in the loop. Cognition AI hit a [$10.2B valuation](https://cognition.ai/blog/devin-can-now-schedule-devins) by solving one problem exceptionally well: give a founder or an engineering team a developer that never sleeps.

The comparison to Soleur comes up because both are positioned as AI autonomy for builders. But Devin and Soleur are not competing for the same job. They operate at different altitudes.

Devin is an engineer. Soleur is an organization.

Understanding that distinction — and when it matters — is the entire comparison.

## What Devin Actually Is

Devin is Cognition AI's autonomous software engineering agent. It can read a GitHub issue, explore the codebase, write a fix, run tests, and open a pull request without human prompting. As of March 2026, Cognition shipped two significant expansions:

- **Scheduled Devins** — Devin can now schedule its own recurring sessions, running nightly or weekly tasks like dependency audits, test coverage reports, and changelog maintenance without user prompts
- **Managed Devins** — Complex tasks decompose across parallel Devin workers, each handling a subtask and merging results

These are meaningful capabilities. Scheduled Devins with cross-session state carry context between runs — the closest any engineering agent has come to a compounding knowledge model. Within engineering, Devin is approaching genuine institutional memory.

The scope stays fixed. Every Devin capability targets software: code, tests, documentation, repositories. There is no legal reasoning. No financial modeling. No marketing strategy. No product spec generation. Devin does not know what a margin is, and it should not. The single-domain focus is intentional.

## What Soleur Actually Is

Soleur is a Company-as-a-Service platform. {{ stats.agents }} agents and {{ stats.skills }} skills, organized across {{ stats.departments }} departments: engineering, marketing, legal, finance, operations, product, sales, and support. Every agent carries domain knowledge. Every workflow produces committed artifacts that compound into institutional memory across the entire organization — not just within one function.

The workflow lifecycle is explicit: brainstorm → plan → implement → review → compound. The compound step is the structural difference. Where Devin's state carries between engineering sessions, Soleur's compounding carries across departments. A legal decision informs a product spec. A competitive intelligence audit shapes a marketing campaign. A finance constraint changes a roadmap priority. Cross-domain coherence is architecture, not accident.

Soleur runs in Claude Code. It is open-source and free.

## The Engineering Layer Is Not the Full Company

Devin solves a real and expensive problem. Hiring a senior engineer costs $180,000–$300,000 per year in the US. An autonomous agent that can handle sprint-ready tickets, write documentation, maintain CI pipelines, and triage issues represents genuine leverage for a solo founder.

The problem is that engineering is one of eight functions required to run a company.

Consider what Devin cannot do on the day your largest customer asks for a data processing agreement:

- Analyze your data flows and identify GDPR Article 28 processor obligations
- Draft a DPA compliant with your existing privacy policy and applicable regulations
- Flag clauses in the customer's proposed DPA that conflict with your current SaaS terms
- Recommend negotiation positions based on deal size and customer tier

None of this is engineering. All of it blocks the sale. Soleur's legal agents handle exactly this workflow, with output tracked against the knowledge base from previous legal reviews and coordinated with finance and product context.

The same gap appears in every domain. Devin cannot build a positioning brief for your next launch. It cannot model a pricing change against your current customer cohorts. It cannot run a product brainstorm against your competitive intelligence and your brand guide simultaneously. These are not engineering tasks. They are the rest of the company.

## Where Devin Wins

Devin is the correct choice when the bottleneck is software delivery itself:

- **High-volume engineering backlogs** — Devin processes large queues of well-specified tickets faster than most human engineers
- **Automated maintenance** — Dependency updates, security patches, test coverage, and documentation are Devin's native environment; Scheduled Devins make this continuous
- **Parallel execution** — Managed Devins decompose large refactors across parallel workers, compressing engineering timelines
- **Engineering-first teams** — If the founders are technical and every bottleneck is in the codebase, Devin's depth-over-breadth design is the right fit

## Where Soleur Wins

Soleur is the correct choice when the bottleneck is organizational intelligence, not code output:

- **Cross-domain decisions** — Product strategy requires legal, finance, and engineering context simultaneously. Soleur agents share a knowledge base; Devin cannot reason across departments
- **High-stakes non-engineering work** — Legal compliance, financial modeling, and sales pipeline analysis require domain expertise Devin is not designed to provide
- **Knowledge compounding** — Soleur's compound step turns every session into durable institutional memory. A solo founder using Soleur for six months builds a knowledge base that reflects their specific company, market, and decisions. Devin's context window clears after each engineering session
- **Founder-in-the-loop workflows** — Every Soleur workflow has explicit decision gates. The founder approves direction, not implementation details. Devin operates autonomously within engineering; Soleur amplifies founder judgment across the organization

## The Price Anchor Problem

Devin's pricing established a market expectation for autonomous agents. That figure becomes misleading when used as the unit of comparison for a full organization.

A solo founder replacing their team needs more than an engineer. They need a lawyer, a marketer, a product manager, a CFO, and a head of sales — at minimum. Measured against that replacement cost, the relevant comparison is not Devin's monthly price vs. Soleur's price. It is Devin's monthly price plus every other tool or hire the founder still needs.

Soleur is free and open-source. The only cost is your Claude Code subscription.

## Using Both

Devin and Soleur do not compete for the same slot in a founder's stack. A solo founder who needs deep autonomous engineering velocity can use Devin for the codebase and Soleur for everything else. Soleur's engineering agents handle architecture decisions, code reviews, and agentic engineering workflows from inside Claude Code. Devin's autonomous execution handles the ticket queue.

If engineering throughput is the constraint, Devin is worth evaluating. If organizational intelligence is the constraint — and for most solo founders, it is both — Soleur addresses the part Devin was never designed to cover.

The question is not which is better. It is which layer you are missing.
