# Value Proposition Framing Test — Findings

**Date:** 2026-03-26
**Method:** Synthetic persona reactions to three value proposition framings
**Personas:** 10 solo founder archetypes across revenue, technical depth, AI attitude, and pain-point dimensions

## Framings Tested

| ID | Headline | Core Pitch |
|----|----------|------------|
| A — CaaS | "Your AI company" | AI organization with departments sharing a compounding knowledge base |
| B — Pain-Point | "Stop hiring, start delegating" | You're doing 8 jobs, Soleur handles 7 of them |
| C — Tool-Replacement | "One platform, 8 departments" | Replace your $765-3,190/month tool stack with one $49/month platform |

---

## Per-Persona Reactions

### 1. Marcus (Pre-revenue SaaS, deep tech, Claude Code user, enthusiast)

**Winner: Framing B** (Pain-Point)

Marcus already uses Claude Code daily and believes AI can let solo founders compete with funded teams. "Stop hiring, start delegating" hits his exact situation: he's a great engineer drowning in legal, marketing, and pricing decisions he keeps postponing.

- **Framing A (CaaS):** Intrigued but detached. "AI organization" sounds conceptually cool to him as a tech enthusiast, but it doesn't answer the question that keeps him up at night: "Can this actually write my terms of service and tell me how to price my product?" The abstraction is interesting in a blog-post way, not in a "shut up and take my money" way.
- **Framing B (Pain-Point):** Immediate recognition. "You're doing 8 jobs" — he laughed at this because he tracks his time and knows it's true. The phrase "delegated to AI agents that remember everything" resonates because his current workflow is re-prompting Claude with the same context repeatedly. "This is what I've been trying to build with Claude sessions already."
- **Framing C (Tool-Replacement):** Weak — Marcus is pre-revenue. He's spending $0-50/month on tools. The cost-savings angle doesn't apply. He's not replacing a stack; he doesn't have one yet.

**Top objection:** "What does 'handles' actually mean? If I delegate my legal setup, does it produce real legal documents or just brainstorming?"

**Representative quote:** "I've been trying to duct-tape this together with Claude already. If this actually remembers my product context across marketing and legal, that's what I need. But show me the output, not the org chart."

### 2. Priya ($3K MRR dev tools, deep tech, Cursor user, pragmatist)

**Winner: Framing B** (Pain-Point)

Priya is drowning in a specific, high-stakes domain (GDPR, DPAs, takedown notices) and already spent $800 on a single 30-minute lawyer call. She's skeptical about AI for legal because "the stakes are too high to trust a chatbot." The framing that acknowledges she's overloaded — without claiming to replace expert judgment — has the best chance.

- **Framing A (CaaS):** Active resistance. "Your AI company" triggers her legal-skepticism alarm. She doesn't want an "AI legal department" — she wants help processing the DPA requests that are piling up so she can focus on code. The abstraction feels like overselling.
- **Framing B (Pain-Point):** Partial resonance. "You're doing 8 jobs" lands — she can enumerate them. But "Soleur handles 7 of them" feels overclaimed. She would accept "Soleur handles the first 80% of 7 of them so you can focus on the 20% that requires your judgment." The phrase "legal contracts" in the feature list catches her eye specifically.
- **Framing C (Tool-Replacement):** Irrelevant. She uses Cursor, Linear, and Stripe — all developer tools she chose deliberately. She's not looking to replace Cursor with an all-in-one. The consolidation pitch doesn't match her mental model.

**Top objection:** "You say 'legal contracts' — are these enforceable? Who reviewed them? If I send a Soleur-generated DPA to a German enterprise customer and it's wrong, I'm the one getting fined, not you."

**Representative quote:** "I don't need an AI legal department. I need something that drafts the DPA so I'm not starting from a blank page before I send it to a real lawyer. If it does that, maybe. If it claims to replace the lawyer, no."

### 3. Elena (Pre-revenue creative, moderate tech, no AI tools, skeptic)

**Winner: None landed cleanly** — Framing B came closest but still bounced.

Elena is the hardest sell in this cohort. She's an AI skeptic who tried ChatGPT once and got "generic corporate garbage." She doesn't use AI tools for anything. Her pain is marketing, which is the domain she trusts AI least for creative/strategic work.

- **Framing A (CaaS):** Confusion. "Your AI company" makes her think of autonomous AI agents doing things without her control. As a designer, she values creative control. "I don't want an AI company. I want MY company."
- **Framing B (Pain-Point):** Closest to landing. She resonates with "You're doing 8 jobs" — she is. But "Soleur handles 7 of them" triggers her skeptic reflex. "It can't reach architects at scale for me. That requires relationships, not algorithms." She might click through on this headline, but the pitch underneath would need to show a concrete example of marketing output that doesn't look "generic."
- **Framing C (Tool-Replacement):** Wrong universe. She barely has a tool stack — Canva, Google Docs, and manual coding. The $765-3,190/month number feels like someone else's problem. "I don't spend that much on tools. I spend that much on rent."

**Top objection:** "Show me one marketing campaign this thing would create for selling design templates to architects. If it's the same 'leverage your unique value proposition' garbage ChatGPT gave me, I'm out."

**Representative quote:** "Every AI tool promises to do my marketing. None of them understand that architects don't respond to LinkedIn hustle culture. They respond to portfolio quality and word of mouth. How does your AI know that?"

### 4. James ($15K MRR e-commerce, deep tech, pragmatist)

**Winner: Framing B** (Pain-Point)

James is spending 25+ hours per week on operations — inventory, suppliers, shipping, returns, support — and he's a systems engineer who knows this doesn't scale. He hasn't tried AI for business tasks because "I don't have time to experiment with another tool that probably won't work."

- **Framing A (CaaS):** Intellectually interesting but feels like a pitch deck, not a product. "AI organization with departments" — James thinks in systems, not departments. He wants to know: does this integrate with Shopify? ShipStation? Zendesk? The CaaS framing doesn't signal operational integration.
- **Framing B (Pain-Point):** Direct hit. "You're doing 8 jobs" — he's doing more than 8. "Soleur handles 7 of them" — he immediately asks "which 7?" The feature list (marketing campaigns, competitive analysis, financial planning) is interesting but misses his biggest pain: supplier communications, shipping logistics, returns. Still, the framing is right even if the feature list needs adjustment.
- **Framing C (Tool-Replacement):** Strong second. James IS spending $765+/month on tools (Shopify, ShipStation, Zendesk, QuickBooks, Copilot). The cost math is real for him. But he immediately goes to: "Can it actually replace Zendesk? Or is it a wrapper that doesn't handle the edge cases?" The tool-replacement claim invites skepticism about depth.

**Top objection:** "Does this integrate with my existing stack or does it want me to rip-and-replace? Because I'm not migrating off Shopify."

**Representative quote:** "Twenty-five hours a week on ops that should be automated. If this handles supplier follow-ups and returns processing, I'll try it tonight. But 'one platform, 8 departments' sounds like it does everything badly."

### 5. Sofia ($1K MRR consulting SaaS, low tech, ChatGPT user, enthusiast)

**Winner: Framing A** (CaaS)

Sofia is the surprise CaaS convert. As a non-technical founder who already uses ChatGPT daily and calls it "the only way I can compete," the idea of having an AI organization clicks for her. She's terrified of legal/compliance and has no idea how to handle it — so the idea that there's a "legal department" she can talk to, even an AI one, is psychologically comforting.

- **Framing A (CaaS):** Resonance. "Your AI company" maps to how she already thinks about ChatGPT — as a team member she can talk to. "Departments for legal, finance" — this is exactly what she wishes she had. She doesn't have the technical skepticism that makes deeper founders resist the abstraction. The "compounding knowledge base" part went over her head, though.
- **Framing B (Pain-Point):** Also strong. "You're doing 8 jobs" resonates. But "stop hiring" doesn't land perfectly because she's never considered hiring — she can't afford it. "Start delegating" is better because that's what she already does with ChatGPT. Close second.
- **Framing C (Tool-Replacement):** Moderate. She pays for Bubble, Notion, ChatGPT, and Stripe — maybe $200/month. The savings math works but isn't dramatic enough to drive a decision. She's more motivated by capability gaps (legal, compliance) than cost reduction.

**Top objection:** "If I ask the legal department about a DPA and it gives me something wrong, who's responsible? I'm storing client financial data. A mistake here isn't an oops, it's a lawsuit."

**Representative quote:** "I already treat ChatGPT like a team. If this is a better team that actually remembers my product and my compliance requirements — yes. But 'departments' better mean it actually knows legal, not that it guesses legal."

## Framing A: CaaS ("Your AI company") — Aggregate Analysis

### Who it resonated with

- **Sofia** (enthusiast, low-tech, ChatGPT user) — the only clear win. She already anthropomorphizes ChatGPT as a team member. "Departments" maps to her aspirational self-image: a real company with real departments, even if they're AI. The CaaS frame fills a psychological need, not just a functional one.
- **Partial interest from Rafael and Aisha** — both noticed "legal department" or "finance department" as matching their specific gap, but the broader CaaS wrapper didn't add value beyond the specific department they cared about.

### Who it confused

- **Elena, Min-Ji** (skeptics, no AI tools) — both heard "AI company" and recoiled. Elena: "I want MY company." Min-Ji: "I have a real company. I need customers." The framing felt like it was replacing the founder, not helping them.
- **Rafael** (low-tech, pragmatist) — the organizational metaphor didn't match his task-oriented mental model. He thinks "I need a template," not "I need a department."
- **Tobias** (deep tech/PhD) — saw through the abstraction immediately. "This is role-playing with system prompts."

### Common objection patterns

1. **"What does 'department' actually mean in practice?"** — The CaaS framing is a category label, not a capability description. Most personas couldn't map "department" to a concrete action they'd take on Day 1.
2. **"Are you replacing me or helping me?"** — The framing's biggest risk is an identity threat. Founders identify AS their company. "Your AI company" implies a parallel entity, not an augmentation.
3. **"Novel category = burden of explanation on you"** — Every persona who didn't immediately get it would require education. That's a marketing tax on every acquisition channel.

## Framing B: Pain-Point ("Stop hiring, start delegating") — Aggregate Analysis

### Who it resonated with

- **Marcus, James, Derek, Aisha, Rafael, Tobias** — six of ten personas. The "doing 8 jobs" hook achieved near-universal recognition because it's a literal description of solo founder life, not a metaphor.
- **Derek** strongest of all — "stop hiring" is his literal decision point this quarter. The framing intercepted an active buying trigger.
- **Sofia** — strong second place behind CaaS for her. "Start delegating" matches her existing ChatGPT behavior.

### Who it bounced off

- **Elena, Min-Ji** (skeptics) — both agreed with the problem diagnosis ("doing 8 jobs") but rejected the solution claim ("handles 7 of them"). Their prior AI experiences gave them reason to doubt the capability assertion. The framing opened the door but the pitch overpromised.
- **Priya** (pragmatist with high-stakes domain) — partial resonance but the "handles 7" claim triggered her accuracy anxiety. She'd need the framing softened to "handles the first draft of 7."

### Common objection patterns

1. **"Handles HOW? Show me the output."** — The framing is great at problem recognition but vague on mechanism. Every persona who liked it immediately asked: "Okay, but show me what 'handling legal' actually produces."
2. **"7 of them is overclaimed"** — Multiple personas (Priya, Tobias, Min-Ji) found "handles 7 of them" to be a credibility stretch. The number invites enumeration, and enumeration invites skepticism about each item.
3. **"Which 7?"** — The generic feature list (marketing, legal, competitive analysis, financial planning) doesn't match every persona's pain. James wants operations, Aisha wants finance, Tobias wants marketing for accountants. The framing needs to allow the reader to self-select their "7."

## Framing C: Tool-Replacement ("One platform, 8 departments") — Aggregate Analysis

### Who it resonated with

- **Derek** — the only persona where tool costs are high enough ($500+/month) and pain is broad enough that consolidation is genuinely attractive. Even for him, "cross-domain intelligence" was the hook, not "save money."
- **James** — moderate interest. He's spending meaningfully on tools and the math is real, but his objection ("does it replace Zendesk or half-replace it?") killed the momentum.

### Who it confused or alienated

- **Pre-revenue founders (Marcus, Elena, Tobias, Rafael)** — four personas have no meaningful tool spend. The $765-3,190/month comparison number felt like a different planet. "That's not my problem."
- **Min-Ji, Sofia** — low tool spend ($100-200/month). The savings math ($49 vs. their current spend) isn't dramatic enough to drive behavior change.
- **Priya** — developer-tool loyalist. She chose Cursor and Linear deliberately. "Replace" is a threat, not a feature.

### Common objection patterns

1. **"Replace implies parity, and I don't believe that"** — The tool-replacement frame invites direct feature comparison against established tools. No persona believed Soleur could match Zendesk at support, QuickBooks at accounting, AND Cursor at development simultaneously.
2. **"$765-3,190/month — who is this for?"** — The price range felt targeted at a later-stage founder than most of the cohort. Six of ten personas spend under $300/month on tools.
3. **"I don't want to consolidate, I want to fill gaps"** — Most solo founders don't have tool sprawl — they have tool gaps. They're not using 8 mediocre tools; they're using 2-3 good tools and doing 5 other jobs manually. Consolidation isn't the mental model; capability addition is.

## Winner and Segment Variations

### Overall winner: Framing B (Pain-Point)

Framing B won or tied for first with 7 of 10 personas. The problem-recognition hook ("You're doing 8 jobs") is nearly universal among solo founders. It describes their lived experience without requiring them to learn a new concept (CaaS) or do cost math (tool-replacement).

However, Framing B's pitch needs significant refinement — the "handles 7 of them" claim is its biggest liability.

### By segment

| Segment | Best Framing | Why |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| **Deep tech, AI enthusiast** (Marcus, Derek) | B — Pain-Point | They already believe AI can help. The framing just needs to match their specific pain. They don't need to be sold on AI, they need to be sold on scope. |
| **Deep tech, pragmatist** (Priya, James, Tobias) | B — Pain-Point (softened) | They need the claim dialed back. "Handles 7" should become "helps you tackle 7" or "gives you a starting point on 7." Pragmatists buy on demonstrated capability, not promised capability. |
| **Low/moderate tech, enthusiast** (Sofia) | A — CaaS | Uniquely, the organizational metaphor works for non-technical enthusiasts who already treat AI as a team member. This is a small but real segment. |
| **Low/moderate tech, pragmatist** (Aisha, Rafael) | B — Pain-Point | They have specific, urgent needs (finance, legal) and want to know if those specific needs are addressed. The pain-point framing opens the door; the feature list needs to close it. |
| **Skeptics** (Elena, Min-Ji) | None — all three bounced | Skeptics need proof, not framing. They've been burned by AI promises. No headline will convert them; they need to see real output from a comparable use case before they'll engage. Case studies and demos, not taglines. |

### What's missing from all three framings

1. **Specificity.** All three framings are horizontal ("8 departments," "7 of them," "$765-3,190"). Solo founders experience pain vertically — "I need a DPA template" or "I need to know where accountants find software." A framing that lets the reader immediately see THEIR specific pain addressed would outperform all three.

2. **The "memory" differentiator is buried.** The single feature that generated genuine excitement across multiple personas — "AI agents that remember everything" / "compounding knowledge base" — is a subordinate clause, not the headline. For founders currently re-prompting ChatGPT with context every session, persistent memory is the killer feature, not the department structure.

3. **Trust scaffolding.** None of the framings address the accuracy/trust objection that came up with 8 of 10 personas. Phrases like "lawyer-reviewed templates," "human-in-the-loop," or "starting point, not final answer" would dramatically reduce the top objection across the board.

4. **The hiring-cost comparison.** Framing C compares against tool costs. But the real comparison for most founders is against hiring costs. Derek at $30K MRR is weighing a $60-80K/year hire. Even Marcus at $0 MRR knows a lawyer costs $400/hour. "$49/month vs. your first hire" is a more powerful cost frame than "$49/month vs. your SaaS stack."

## Key Observations

### The problem-recognition hook is universal; the solution claim is where framings die

Every persona responded to "You're doing 8 jobs." Not one persona disputed that diagnosis. But every framing lost personas on the solution claim. The distance between "I see myself in this problem" and "I believe this product solves it" is where all three framings have a gap. The fix is not a better framing — it's better proof.

### CaaS is a founder-education problem, not a product-messaging strategy

CaaS worked for exactly one persona (Sofia) and partially for two others. The abstraction requires the reader to already believe AI can run business departments — a belief held by enthusiasts but actively rejected by pragmatists and skeptics. Launching with CaaS as the primary frame means educating the market before selling to it. That's a luxury bootstrapped companies don't have.

### Skeptics are not reachable by value-prop framing alone

Elena and Min-Ji both bounced off all three framings. Their prior negative AI experiences mean they've pre-rejected the category. Reaching them requires: (a) a warm referral from a founder they trust, (b) a free trial with output from their specific domain, or (c) a case study showing AI output that contradicts their "generic garbage" mental model. No headline solves this.

### The "memory" feature is the moat, but no framing leads with it

The single most differentiated capability — context that persists and compounds across departments — generated unprompted positive reactions from Marcus ("that's what I've been trying to build"), Derek ("my biggest Claude frustration is re-explaining context"), Rafael ("I'm re-explaining my licensing model every session"), and Sofia ("remembers my compliance requirements"). This is the feature that separates Soleur from "just use ChatGPT." Yet in all three framings, it's a throwaway phrase at the end of a sentence. Consider testing a fourth framing that leads with memory: "The AI that already knows your business."

### Tool-replacement is a losing frame for early-stage founders

Six of ten personas are pre-revenue or under $3K MRR. They don't have tool sprawl — they have tool absence. Framing C's entire premise (you're overspending on tools) is wrong for this segment. The frame only works for $15K+ MRR founders with established stacks, and even they objected to the "replace" claim. If the ICP is early-stage solo founders, tool-replacement should be retired or repositioned as a secondary message for later-stage segments.

### The hire-vs-AI decision point is the highest-intent trigger

Derek's reaction was the strongest in the entire cohort because he's at an active fork: hire or don't hire. "Stop hiring, start delegating" intercepted a real decision with real dollars attached. This suggests the ideal customer for launch is not pre-revenue founders (who have no budget urgency) but $10K-50K MRR solo founders who are staring at their first hire and looking for a way to avoid it. The messaging should target that moment specifically.

### Pragmatists need the claim dialed back; enthusiasts need it dialed up

Pragmatists (Priya, James, Tobias, Aisha, Rafael) consistently objected to absolute claims — "handles 7," "replace your stack." They'd respond better to language of assistance: "drafts your legal contracts," "builds your first marketing plan," "organizes your finances." Enthusiasts (Marcus, Sofia, Derek) responded to ambitious claims because they already believe. A single framing can't serve both — the landing page needs progressive disclosure where the hook is conservative and the deep-dive is ambitious.
