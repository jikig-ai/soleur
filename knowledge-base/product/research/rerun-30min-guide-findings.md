## WTP Sequence Assessment

The Q13 -> Q14 anchoring sequence is the structural change with the highest impact on data quality.

### Mechanism

Q13 establishes the cost of the problem in the founder's own words and units. Q14 references that cost and asks what portion a tool would need to cover. This creates a natural price anchor from the founder's own pain rather than asking them to generate a price from nothing.

### Results by Segment

**Pre-revenue founders (Marcus, Elena, Tobias, Rafael):**

- Original average WTP: $79/month (with Tobias at $0)
- Updated average WTP: $325/month (with Tobias at $300/month post-revenue)
- Improvement: 4.1x increase in stated WTP
- Every pre-revenue founder who previously said "I don't know" or "$50" now gives a justified, specific range anchored on real costs

**Early-revenue founders ($1-3K MRR: Priya, Sofia, Min-Ji):**

- Original average WTP: $100/month
- Updated average WTP: $258/month
- Improvement: 2.6x increase
- Sofia's jump from $50 to $225 is driven entirely by the enterprise-deals-declined anchor from Q13

**Growth founders ($8K+ MRR: James, Aisha, Derek):**

- Original average WTP: $867/month
- Updated average WTP: $817/month
- Change: Negligible (-6%)
- These founders were already anchored on known alternative costs (hire price, consultant fees) in the original. The sequence confirms rather than changes their WTP.

### Key Insight

The anchoring sequence has its greatest impact on the founders who were most underserved by the original guide -- pre-revenue and early-revenue founders. Growth founders already had internal price anchors (they know what a hire costs, what a consultant charges). The sequence gives pre-revenue founders the same anchoring mechanism by surfacing non-monetary costs (delay, risk, competitive window) and converting them to economic terms.

### Risk: Anchoring Bias

The sequence could inflate WTP if founders overstate costs in Q13 and then Q14 locks them into a high anchor. Mitigation: Q14 asks "how much would a tool need to COVER" not "how much would you pay." This frames the answer as cost displacement, not willingness to pay. Founders naturally discount: "If a tool covered half of that..." is the common pattern.

### Questions That Still Fall Flat

1. **Q9 ("What would change if handled automatically?")** -- still 40% rich. Tobias still objects to the "automatically" framing. Consider rewriting to: "What would change if you didn't have to think about [domain] anymore?" Removes the mechanism ("automatically") and focuses on the outcome (cognitive load removal).

2. **Q12 ("What would the tool need to do?")** -- still 50% rich, with Tobias and Min-Ji flat. This is the same failure mode from the original: founders who don't understand a domain can't spec tools for it. Q12a (how would you know if it was done wrong) partially compensates but Q12 itself may need a rewrite for marketing-pain personas. Consider: "Describe the result you need, not the tool. What would success look like in 30 days?"

3. **Q8 ("Have you tried AI?")** -- still 1 flat (Min-Ji). This is persona-specific and doesn't warrant a rewrite. Q8a compensates fully by broadening to all alternatives.

### New Problems Introduced

1. **Guide length**: 15 questions in 30 minutes is tight -- approximately 2 minutes per question. The 5 new questions add genuine signal, but the interviewer will need to triage. Recommendation: mark Q6, Q7, Q8a, Q12a, Q15 as "ask if time permits" or split into a 45-minute guide.

2. **Redundancy between Q6 and Q5**: Q5 asks "least qualified" and Q6 asks "what should you be doing but aren't?" For avoidance-pain personas, these overlap. For burden-pain personas, they're distinct (Q5 = what they're doing badly, Q6 = what they're not doing at all). Net: keep both but expect interviewer to skip Q6 when Q5 already covered avoidance pain.

3. **Q15 mismatch for marketing personas**: Elena and Tobias give moderate responses because marketing inherently doesn't resolve in 30 days. The question creates a false urgency that doesn't match their pain timeline. Consider a variant for marketing personas: "What would need to happen for you to have a working marketing engine in 90 days?"

4. **Emotional fatigue risk**: Q5 (competence gap) -> Q6 (avoidance) -> Q7 (fear) is three consecutive questions that probe weakness and anxiety. In a real interview, this sequence could make founders defensive or despondent. Consider inserting a lighter question between Q6 and Q7, or moving Q7 later in the guide.

### Final Verdict

The updated guide is a substantial improvement. The three rewritten questions (Q5, Q13, Q14) eliminate all 13 flat responses they produced in the original run. The five new questions surface pain categories the original guide missed entirely: avoidance mechanisms, emotional weight, failed alternatives, quality evaluation gaps, and purchase urgency. The expanded domain checklist adds value for 7 of 10 personas.

The Q13-Q14 anchoring sequence is the single highest-impact structural change. It transforms willingness-to-pay data from unusable (5 of 10 flat) to universally rich, with pre-revenue founders providing 4x higher WTP when anchored on their own stated costs.

Recommended next steps:

3. Adopt Q7 and Q15 as conditional questions -- strong but may need time-management
