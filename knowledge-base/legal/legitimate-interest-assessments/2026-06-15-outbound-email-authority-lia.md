# Legitimate Interest Assessment — Outbound Email Authority (`mail.soleur.ai`)

This LIA documents the Art. 6(1)(f) three-part test for an **agent-callable,
human-approval-gated, cold outbound email** capability for founder 1:1
outreach (Article 30 register Processing Activity 28; feature
`feat-agent-native-outbound-email`, issue #5325; the #5314 listicle-author
campaign is the inciting use). This activity is the **outbound counterpart**
to PA-27 (inbound triage) and it **deliberately overturns** the deferral
recorded in the 2026-06-11 operator-inbox-triage LIA, where "any reply/send
authority added to the pipeline" was an explicit Non-Goal and a stated
re-evaluation trigger. That trigger has now fired; this LIA is the re-run it
required. **Brand-survival threshold: single-user incident.** CPO sign-off is
a precondition to enabling the capability.

The processing pipeline: a founder/agent-drafted body → a `gated`-tier tool
(`email_send`, `email_reply`, `email_suppress`) surfaces the **exact**
recipient + body to the operator in a review gate → operator approves (the
approval is body-hash-bound and single-use) → the single compliance chokepoint
`server/email-triage/outbound.ts` `sendCompliantOutbound` runs the ordered,
refuse-to-send gate (domain-verified precondition → CLO conditions C1–C5 →
RFC-5322 header-injection guard → recipient allow-list → body-hash approval
match → in-transaction suppression recheck → Resend send → WORM audit row) →
Resend (eu-west-1) sends from the dedicated subdomain `mail.soleur.ai`
(SPF/DKIM/DMARC, p=quarantine→reject) — a separate sender-reputation stream,
distinct from the `soleur.ai` apex transactional sender
(`notifications@soleur.ai`); same registrable domain, distinct subdomain.
**No LLM sits in the send path.** Two new tables
(migration 104) hold only keyed-pseudonymised state: `email_suppression`
(per-founder permanent suppression set) and `outbound_sends` (WORM cold-send
audit). The plaintext recipient address is **never** persisted; bodies are
**never** persisted (only `sha256(body)` hashes).

Per ICO and CNIL guidance on Art. 6(1)(f), a legitimate interest assessment
must address three limbs: **purpose**, **necessity**, and **balancing**. Each
is addressed below in a dedicated `## Purpose` / `## Necessity` /
`## Balancing` H2 section (greppable for AC).

## Purpose

The processing serves a **single, narrow legitimate purpose**:

1. **Enable founder-led 1:1 cold outreach without operating an uncontrolled
   send surface.** Jikigai's survival depends on the founder being able to
   reach a small, hand-picked set of professional contacts (the #5314
   listicle authors — journalists and writers contacted at their *published*
   professional bylines) and conduct a human, one-to-one conversation. The
   interest is the company's legitimate interest in promoting its own
   activities by contacting relevant professionals in their professional
   capacity — recognised by Recital 47 ("the processing of personal data for
   direct marketing purposes may be regarded as carried out for a legitimate
   interest") and by CNIL's professional-contact prospection guidance, with
   the decisive constraint that **every send is individually human-approved**
   and the volume is 1:1, not a campaign blast.

The purpose is **specific, explicit, and legitimate**:

- **Specific:** limited to founder-approved, one-to-one cold outreach to
  professional contacts at published professional addresses; persisted output
  limited to a keyed recipient hash + body hashes + send metadata.
- **Explicit:** stated in advance via this LIA, Article 30 PA-28, the CLO
  conditions C1–C5 enforced at the chokepoint, and the Art. 14 disclosure
  carried in the body of the first EU/UK communication itself (C3).
- **Legitimate:** contacting a named professional at their published
  professional address, once, with a clear identity, purpose, opt-out, and
  Art. 14 disclosure, for a relevant business proposition, is a recognised
  legitimate interest (Recital 47); the human-approval gate keeps it within
  the proportionate, non-bulk envelope that makes it so.

**Not pursued under this LIA:** any **auto-send** of cold mail (the human
approval gate per send is permanent and non-negotiable — there is no
auto-send path, ever); any bulk/campaign sending beyond founder-approved 1:1
outreach; any list rental, purchased lists, or scraped-list ingestion; any
profiling or scoring of recipients; any automated reply/decline-matching
(explicitly **deferred to #5331** — in this increment "suppression" is the
manual/agent `email_suppress` action only); any sending to internal/own-domain
or role/bare addresses (rejected at the chokepoint — see Necessity §recipient
controls). The body is never summarised or sent to an LLM on the outbound leg.

## Necessity

The processing is **necessary** — a less intrusive alternative cannot achieve
the same legitimate purpose, and the design minimises the processing within
the necessary envelope:

1. **Alternative A — founder sends manually from a personal/`soleur.ai`
   mailbox (status quo):** Rejected. It provides **no** structural guarantee
   of the Art. 14 disclosure, the postal-address footer, the FTC
   material-connection disclosure, or permanent opt-out suppression; it
   commingles cold outreach with the transactional `soleur.ai` sender domain
   (deliverability and reputation risk to the product domain); and it leaves
   no tamper-evident accountability record. The chokepoint exists precisely
   because "a human typing into a mailbox" cannot be relied on to satisfy
   C1–C5 on every send.

2. **Alternative B — an open agent send capability (no per-send human gate):**
   Rejected as disproportionate and unsafe at the brand-survival threshold.
   An agent that can send cold mail autonomously is an exfiltration and
   reputational primitive (a single hallucinated or injected recipient/body
   is a cold email to a real third party from the company's verified domain).
   The **permanent human-approval gate is the decisive safeguard** that keeps
   this within the 1:1, founder-curated envelope the purpose depends on. The
   approval is **body-hash-bound and single-use**: the chokepoint recomputes
   `sha256(body)` and compares it to the approved hash, so a body mutated
   after approval is refused — the human approved *that* exact text, not a
   category of text.

3. **Alternative C — persist plaintext recipients and bodies for audit
   quality:** Rejected. **Keyed pseudonymisation is the data-minimising
   design:** the plaintext recipient address is never stored; both tables key
   on `recipient_hash = HMAC-SHA-256(EMAIL_HASH_PEPPER, normalize(email))`
   (deterministic keyed pseudonymisation, Art. 32). Bodies are never
   persisted at all — only `approved_body_sha256` and `per_send_body_sha256`,
   which are sufficient to prove *what was approved equalled what was sent*
   without retaining any third-party content on our infrastructure. The
   pepper lives in Doppler (`EMAIL_HASH_PEPPER`), so the hashes are not
   reversible by a database reader.

4. **Alternative D — per-row random salt instead of a keyed HMAC (stronger
   unlinkability):** Rejected, and this is the **deliberate
   pseudonymisation tradeoff** that counsel must confirm. The suppression
   guarantee (C5: a permanent, monotonic opt-out set checked at send time and
   in-transaction) **requires** that a recipient seen in a future outreach be
   matchable against the suppression set *without* ever holding the plaintext
   address. A per-row salt would make two encounters of the same address
   produce different hashes, **foreclosing cross-campaign suppression
   matching** — i.e. it would break the very data-subject-protective control
   (never email someone who opted out) that the design exists to guarantee.
   The keyed HMAC is therefore *linkable by design*, and that linkability is
   necessary and proportionate: it is confined to the suppression/audit
   purpose, the key is a Doppler secret (not a per-row value a DB reader can
   recompute), and the alternative is *weaker* protection for the data
   subject, not stronger.

5. **Single chokepoint, refuse-to-send by construction.** All cold sends go
   through exactly one function (`sendCompliantOutbound` in
   `server/email-triage/outbound.ts`); there is no second send path. Every
   gate **throws before Resend is called** (pure refuse-to-send), in this
   fixed order: domain-verified precondition → CLO C1–C5 → RFC-5322
   header-injection guard → recipient allow-list → body-hash approval match →
   in-transaction suppression recheck → Resend → WORM audit row. This is the
   minimisation control: mail that fails any predicate is never transmitted to
   a third party at all.

6. **CLO conditions C1–C5 as the substantive minimisation/transparency floor.**
   C1 physical postal-address footer; C2 opt-out line; **C3 the EU/UK Art. 14
   disclosure, validated as SIX discrete element predicates (identity,
   purpose, legal-basis = legitimate-interest, data source/category,
   retention, rights) — mechanical presence only**; C4 FTC material-connection
   disclosure; C5 permanent opt-out suppression (send-time corollary:
   refuse-to-send if suppressed). **C3 is how the Art. 14 obligation is
   discharged at first contact** (see Balancing §(iii)): because the recipient
   data was not obtained from them, Art. 14 information must be provided, and
   the design provides it *in the first communication itself* rather than via
   a separate notice. The chokepoint enforces only *mechanical presence* of
   the six predicates; **semantic correctness is owned by the human approver**,
   which is sound precisely because every cold send is human-approved (no
   unattended send can ship a structurally-present-but-meaningless
   disclosure).

7. **Recipient controls (anti-exfiltration minimisation).** `email_reply`'s
   recipient is derived **server-side** from the persisted inbound item's
   sender — never from agent-supplied arguments — so the agent cannot redirect
   a reply. Internal / own-domain addresses (`*@soleur.ai`, `*@mail.soleur.ai`,
   `*@jikigai.com`) and role/bare addresses are rejected by the allow-list. Jurisdiction
   **defaults to EU/UK-strict** when unknown or low-confidence (never a lenient
   US fallthrough), so the stricter disclosure regime applies whenever there
   is any doubt.

8. **Retention proportional to purpose.** `email_suppression` is monotonic and
   permanent by design (no un-suppress) — its retention IS the opt-out
   guarantee (an opt-out that expired would re-expose the data subject).
   `outbound_sends` is append-only WORM audit retained for the accountability
   period (Art. 5(2)) as third-party-facing evidence that each cold send was
   human-approved and compliant. **Vendor-side:** Resend retains sent content
   30 days with no deletion API — the same finding as PA-27 (disclosed, not
   controllable; the window simply expires). **Art. 17 path:**
   `anonymise_email_suppression` + `anonymise_outbound_sends` (service-role
   only, called by `account-delete.ts` before auth deletion; NULL `owner_id` +
   scrub `recipient_hash`). There is **no self-service erasure** of the
   outbound audit — it is third-party-facing accountability evidence under
   Art. 5(2), retained for the accountability period (the same Art. 17(3)(b)
   logic as PA-27's statutory rows; counsel item 3 below).

**Conclusion:** the single-chokepoint, refuse-to-send, human-approval-gated,
keyed-pseudonymised, body-never-persisted design is the least intrusive
architecture that achieves founder-led 1:1 cold outreach while structurally
guaranteeing the Art. 14 disclosure (C3), permanent opt-out (C5), and
anti-exfiltration recipient controls. Each rejected alternative is either less
protective of the data subject or fails the purpose.

## Balancing

Per WP29 Opinion 06/2014 §III.3, the balancing test considers: (i) nature of
the interests, (ii) nature of the data, (iii) reasonable expectations,
(iv) impact, (v) safeguards. The decisive feature of this activity is that the
recipients are **involuntary, third-party data subjects whose contact details
were collected NOT from them** (e.g. a journalist's published byline), and who
have no account, contract, or prior relationship with Jikigai. This is *cold*
contact, so the balance must be earned by the safeguards, not assumed.

### (i) Nature of the controller's interests

- A legitimate commercial interest in promoting the company's activities by
  contacting relevant professionals (Recital 47), at the brand-survival
  threshold (single-user incident). It is a genuine, present, and specific
  interest — a named, hand-picked set of contacts for a named campaign
  (#5314), not speculative bulk prospecting.

### (ii) Nature of the data and data subjects

- The data is a professional email address (published, professional-capacity)
  plus the founder-drafted body addressed to that person. **No special
  categories** are sought or processed; the body is a business proposition.
- At rest, the controller holds **no plaintext recipient and no body** — only
  `recipient_hash` (keyed HMAC) and body hashes. The data-subject-identifying
  footprint on our infrastructure is structurally a one-way pseudonym.

### (iii) Reasonable expectations of the data subject

- A journalist or professional who **publishes** a contact address as their
  professional byline does so to be contacted in a professional capacity; a
  single, relevant, identified, opt-out-respecting 1:1 message is within the
  reasonable expectations of someone who publishes a professional address —
  this is the expectation the CNIL professional-contact prospection guidance
  and Recital 47 turn on. The expectation does **not** extend to bulk
  campaigns, repeated contact after an opt-out, or contact with no opt-out —
  which is exactly what C2/C5 and the 1:1 human gate prevent.
- **Art. 14 obligation (data not obtained from the data subject).** Because
  the contact details were not obtained from the recipient, Art. 14 applies:
  the controller must provide identity, purposes, legal basis, the categories
  of data and **the source** from which it was obtained, retention, and
  rights — by the time of the first communication (Art. 14(3)(b): "if the
  personal data are to be used for communication with the data subject, at the
  latest at the time of the first communication"). **C3 discharges this in the
  first EU/UK communication itself**, validated as six discrete present
  predicates (identity, purpose, legal-basis = legitimate-interest, data
  source/category, retention, rights). Unlike PA-27's inbound posture (which
  relied on the Art. 14(5)(b) disproportionate-effort exemption because
  individually notifying every involuntary sender was impractical), **here we
  do NOT rely on a disproportionate-effort exemption** — the first contact is
  itself an email to the data subject, so providing the Art. 14 information
  in-line is neither disproportionate nor impossible; it is the natural and
  required posture, and C3 mechanises it. Counsel item 1.

### (iv) Impact on the data subject

- Low-to-moderate, and bounded: a single, identified, opt-out-bearing message
  to a published professional address. The recipient can opt out via C2; the
  opt-out is honoured **permanently** (C5, monotonic suppression with a
  send-time refuse-to-send corollary). There is no profiling, no onward
  publication, no Art. 22 automated decision, and no second message without a
  fresh human approval.
- The materially adverse paths are (a) contacting someone who opted out
  (mitigated structurally by the in-transaction suppression recheck — the
  suppression set is re-read inside the send transaction, not just at approval
  time, so a same-instant opt-out wins), and (b) a wrong/redirected recipient
  (mitigated by server-side reply-recipient derivation, the own-domain /
  role-address rejection, and the body-hash single-use approval binding).

### (v) Safeguards (TOMs — Art. 32 cross-reference, full list in PA-28 §(g))

- **Permanent per-send human-approval gate** — `gated`-tier tools; the
  operator sees the exact recipient + body and approves before anything sends;
  no auto-send for cold mail, ever. This is the decisive safeguard.
- **Body-hash-bound, single-use approval** — `sha256(body)` recomputed at the
  chokepoint vs the approved hash; a post-approval mutation is refused.
- **Single chokepoint, ordered refuse-to-send** — every gate throws before
  Resend (domain-verified → C1–C5 → RFC-5322 header-injection guard →
  allow-list → body-hash match → in-transaction suppression recheck → send →
  WORM row).
- **CLO C1–C5** — postal footer, opt-out, Art. 14 six-predicate disclosure,
  FTC disclosure, permanent suppression (semantic correctness owned by the
  human approver).
- **Anti-exfiltration recipient controls** — server-side reply-recipient
  derivation; own-domain (`*@soleur.ai`, `*@mail.soleur.ai`, `*@jikigai.com`) and role/bare
  address rejection; EU/UK-strict jurisdiction default on low confidence.
- **Keyed-HMAC pseudonymisation at rest** (`recipient_hash`,
  `EMAIL_HASH_PEPPER` in Doppler); plaintext recipient and body never
  persisted; only body hashes stored.
- **WORM `outbound_sends` audit** (append-only, pure-reject trigger,
  owner-RLS) — tamper-evident accountability evidence.
- **PII-free observability** — Sentry mirror carries op + hashed userId only,
  never the recipient or body; because a raw Resend error can echo the
  recipient, the chokepoint passes a **synthetic Error** rather than the raw
  vendor error.

### Art. 17 path for involuntary recipients (and the accountability override)

A recipient may request erasure and/or opt out. The mechanisms:

1. **Opt-out (the primary, immediate control):** `email_suppress` adds the
   recipient to `email_suppression` (monotonic, permanent). At send time C5 +
   the in-transaction suppression recheck refuse-to-send — the opt-out cannot
   be defeated by a later send.
2. **Erasure of identity:** `anonymise_email_suppression` and
   `anonymise_outbound_sends` (service-role only, run by `account-delete.ts`
   before auth deletion) NULL `owner_id` and scrub `recipient_hash`. Because
   the address is held only as a keyed pseudonym, scrubbing the hash removes
   the linkage.
3. **Accountability-period override for the audit:** `outbound_sends` is
   third-party-facing evidence that a given cold send was human-approved and
   carried the C1–C5 disclosures (Art. 5(2)). Like PA-27's statutory rows it
   is retained for the accountability period (the WORM trigger means deletion
   is only via the GUC-gated anonymise/purge path); the
   less-intrusive partial measure (scrub `recipient_hash`, keep the
   compliance-evidence shape) is preferred where the accountability evidence
   survives without the identifier. Counsel item 3.

### Conclusion

The controller's legitimate interest in founder-led, 1:1, human-approved cold
outreach to professionals at their published addresses outweighs the residual
interests of those recipients in not being cold-contacted, **given**: the
published-professional-address expectation, the in-line Art. 14 disclosure at
first contact (C3 — no reliance on a disproportionate-effort exemption), the
permanent opt-out honoured by construction (C5 + in-transaction recheck), the
structurally minimal pseudonymised footprint (no plaintext recipient, no
body), the anti-exfiltration recipient controls, and — decisively — the
permanent per-send human-approval gate that keeps the activity inside the 1:1,
founder-curated envelope on which the whole balance depends.

**Balancing outcome: legitimate interest prevails — conditional on the
permanent human-approval gate and C1–C5 remaining in force.** Art. 6(1)(f) is
the appropriate lawful basis for PA-28. Any removal of the human gate or the
disclosure conditions voids this assessment (see Re-evaluation triggers).
