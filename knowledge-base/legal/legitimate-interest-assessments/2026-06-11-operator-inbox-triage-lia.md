# Legitimate Interest Assessment — Operator Inbox Triage (`ops@soleur.ai`)

This LIA documents the Art. 6(1)(f) three-part test for **AI-assisted triage
of inbound email to company operational addresses** (Article 30 register
Processing Activity 27; feature `feat-operator-inbox-delegation`, issue
#5103). The processing pipeline: Proton Sieve **forward-and-keep** (the
Proton `ops@soleur.ai` mailbox remains the durable original) → Resend Inbound
(US; 30-day vendor-side retention, no deletion API) → svix-verified
metadata-only webhook → Inngest pipeline → deterministic statutory fast-path
(pre-LLM) OR read-only Anthropic summarization → an `email_triage_items` row
containing summary, subject, sender, message-id and received-at — **never the
body** (the table has no body column; migration
`apps/web-platform/supabase/migrations/102_email_triage_items.sql`).

Per ICO and CNIL guidance on Art. 6(1)(f), a legitimate interest assessment
must address three limbs: **purpose**, **necessity**, and **balancing**. Each
is addressed below in a dedicated `## Purpose` / `## Necessity` /
`## Balancing` H2 section (greppable for AC).

## Purpose

The processing serves **two cumulative legitimate purposes**:

1. **Operate the company's inbound-mail function without silent failure.**
   Jikigai operates `ops@soleur.ai` as the company's operational contact
   address. Unread operational mail is an established, materialized risk (the
   2026-06-10 Better Stack quota warning sat unread). The triage pipeline
   converts each inbound email into a decision-ready inbox item with a
   notification ping, so operational mail (vendor notices, billing, security
   advisories) is acted on instead of rotting.

2. **Meet statutory response clocks deterministically.** Some inbound mail
   carries legal deadlines: data-subject requests (Art. 12(3) — one calendar
   month), personal-data-breach notifications (Art. 33 — 72 hours), service
   of process, and supervisory-authority correspondence. The deterministic,
   metadata-first statutory fast-path (code-static registry
   `lib/email-triage/statutory-rules.ts`; catalog
   `knowledge-base/legal/statutory-response-catalog.md`) detects these
   classes BEFORE any LLM involvement and pins them with the clock stated.
   Failing these clocks is itself a GDPR compliance failure; a triage system
   that protects them serves the data subjects whose requests would
   otherwise be missed — the processing partially serves the **same people
   it processes data about**.

The purpose is **specific, explicit, and legitimate**:

- **Specific:** limited to triaging mail addressed to one company operational
  address; persisted output limited to summary + minimal envelope metadata.
- **Explicit:** stated in advance via this LIA, Article 30 PA-27, the DPIA
  screening memo, and the public disclosure in Privacy Policy §4.13 / GDPR
  Policy §3.10 / DPD §2.3(aa).
- **Legitimate:** operating a functioning, legally responsive company mailbox
  is a core operational interest; honoring Art. 12(3)/Art. 33 clocks is a
  statutory interest.

**Not pursued under this LIA:** any outbound reply authority (no send
authority exists in this increment — spec Non-Goal, #4672 boundary), any
profiling of senders, any sender-trust scoring (rejected at review:
Sieve-forwarded sender identity is an unauthenticated claim), and any
processing of attachment content (metadata only; content never downloaded).

## Necessity

The processing is **necessary** — a less intrusive alternative cannot achieve
the same legitimate purpose:

1. **Alternative A — operator reads all mail manually (status quo):**
   Rejected as the failure mode being remediated. The inciting incident class
   (unread quota warning) demonstrates the manual path does not reliably
   deliver either purpose; for statutory mail it is a single human point of
   failure on a 72-hour clock.

2. **Alternative B — mailbox credential access (IMAP via Proton Bridge or
   OAuth):** Rejected. Proton exposes no OAuth/API; Bridge requires a
   send-capable, full-mailbox credential — a strictly larger PII and abuse
   surface than the forwarding architecture, and it would put the operator's
   entire mailbox (not just `ops@`) in scope. The chosen design holds **no
   mailbox credential anywhere**; the only secret is the rotatable webhook
   signing secret.

3. **Alternative C — full-body persistence for better summaries/recall:**
   Rejected. **Parse-and-discard is the data-minimising design:** the body is
   fetched transiently, truncated to 64 KiB, sanitized, summarized, and
   discarded; the schema has no body column, so retention of raw third-party
   content on our infrastructure is structurally impossible. **The Proton
   keep-copy is the data-minimising original store:** because the Sieve rule
   is forward-AND-keep, the durable original lives in the mailbox the company
   already operates (existing processor, existing disclosure surface) instead
   of being duplicated into a second long-term store. When a summary is
   insufficient, the recovery path is the Proton original — not broader
   machine retention.

4. **Alternative D — LLM-only triage (no deterministic fast-path):**
   Rejected. Statutory deadlines must never be gated on a probabilistic
   component; the deterministic registry is what makes purpose 2 reliable,
   and it also minimises processing — statutory and probe mail never reaches
   Anthropic at all.

5. **Data minimisation within the necessary processing:** persisted fields
   are exactly `summary, subject, sender, message-id, resend-email-id,
   received-at (+ provenance), mail/statutory class, rule id, status fields`
   — no body, no full headers, no attachment content, no IP addresses.
   Sub-processor egress is bounded: Anthropic receives only non-statutory
   mail, truncated and sanitized; observability sinks receive no
   subject/sender/body values (`SENSITIVE_KEY_NAMES` scrub + TR3 tri-ban).
   Volume is bounded (throttle 60/h; daily LLM ceiling 200).

6. **Retention proportional to purpose:** probe rows 7 days; non-statutory
   rows 365 days; statutory rows retained for the accountability period
   (Art. 5(1)(e) with the Art. 17(3)(b)-grounded carve-out below). Vendor
   windows that Jikigai cannot shorten are disclosed rather than hidden:
   Resend 30 days (no deletion API), Anthropic 30 days default
   (Zero-Retention amendment tracked in PA-22 (f)), Inngest event store
   self-hosted with no automatic deletion (localhost-bound, EU
   infrastructure; persists until operator-side store maintenance — bound
   tracked as compliance-posture PA-27 Active Items (iv); corrected
   2026-06-11 from "~24h", which is the event-id dedup window, not store
   retention).

**Conclusion:** the forwarding + parse-and-discard + deterministic-fast-path
design is the least intrusive architecture that achieves both purposes;
each rejected alternative is either less protective of data subjects or
fails the statutory-clock purpose.

## Balancing

Per WP29 Opinion 06/2014 §III.3, the balancing test considers: (i) nature of
the interests, (ii) nature of the data, (iii) reasonable expectations,
(iv) impact, (v) safeguards. The decisive feature of this activity is that
the primary data subjects are **involuntary**: inbound senders (and persons
mentioned in their mail) have no account, no contract, and no prior
relationship with the processing.

### (i) Nature of the controller's interests

- Operational interest in a functioning company mailbox (purpose 1).
- Statutory interest in meeting Art. 12(3)/Art. 33 clocks (purpose 2) — an
  interest that directly protects the requesting data subjects.

### (ii) Nature of the data and data subjects

- Senders are predominantly professional correspondents (vendor staff,
  billing systems, regulators) writing to a published company operational
  address in a business context. They are not, as a class, vulnerable data
  subjects — but the address is open: anyone, including private individuals
  and data subjects exercising rights, can write to it, and **special-category
  content may arrive unsolicited**. That residual is named and accepted in
  the DPIA screening memo (residual (a)) rather than claimed away.
- Persisted data is envelope metadata + a 1–3 sentence summary; the raw body
  is structurally excluded from rest.

### (iii) Reasonable expectations of the data subject

- A person emailing a company's operational address reasonably expects the
  company to **read, record, and route** that mail, including with software
  assistance — automated mail handling (filtering, ticketing, CRM ingestion)
  is the norm for business addresses. AI summarization is a modest extension
  of that expectation, and the public disclosure (Privacy Policy §4.13)
  closes the residual transparency gap.
- **Art. 14 information posture (Art. 14(5)(b)):** individually notifying
  every inbound sender that their email was triaged would require emailing
  them back — a contact whose only purpose is the notice itself,
  disproportionate at this scale and intrusive in effect (and impossible for
  persons merely mentioned in mail, whose contact details we do not hold).
  Jikigai instead makes the information publicly available in Privacy Policy
  §4.13 (mirrored in the GDPR Policy and DPD), which names the address
  family, what is stored, the sub-processors, retention, and the rights
  contact (<legal@jikigai.com>). This is the Art. 14(5)(b)
  appropriate-measure posture; counsel review item 2 below.

### (iv) Impact on the data subject

- Low in the typical case: the processing produces an internal triage item
  visible to one operator; no decisions with legal or similar significant
  effect are taken about the sender by automated means (no Art. 22 trigger);
  no onward publication; no profiling.
- The materially adverse paths are mis-summarization (mitigated: the Proton
  original is the system of record for content; the summary is advisory) and
  sub-processor exposure (mitigated: bounded recipients, disclosed windows,
  statutory mail never reaches the LLM).

### (v) Safeguards (TOMs — Art. 32 cross-reference, full list in PA-27 §(g))

- **Structural parse-and-discard** — no body column; body never crosses an
  Inngest step boundary; 64 KiB truncation.
- **Deterministic statutory fast-path before any LLM** + closed
  `mail_class` allowlist (LLM cannot write `statutory_class` or `probe`).
- **WORM mutation matrix** (column-frozen trigger) + GUC-gated SECURITY
  DEFINER RPCs as the only purge/anonymise/status paths.
- **RLS single-owner scoping**; svix signature verification; no mailbox
  credential anywhere.
- **Observability scrub** (`SENSITIVE_KEY_NAMES` + TR3 tri-ban) so
  third-party PII never reaches Sentry/Better Stack.
- **Art. 9 omission instruction** in the summarizer prompt (residual named,
  not claimed solved).
- Retention discipline with a purge that cannot be starved by a broken
  ingress chain (purge-first step order in the daily cron).

### Art. 17 path for involuntary data subjects (and the Art. 17(3)(b) override)

An inbound sender may request erasure of the data we hold about their email.
The mechanism:

1. **What exists to erase:** one `email_triage_items` row (summary, subject,
   sender, message-id, timestamps) per email — never a body. The Proton
   original is handled under the existing mailbox processing; the Resend copy
   expires on the vendor's fixed 30-day window (no deletion API — disclosed,
   not controllable); the Anthropic copy ages out per its 30-day default.
2. **Mechanism:** the WORM trigger rejects ordinary UPDATE/DELETE, so Art. 17
   erasure for involuntary subjects is satisfied by two paths from migration
   102: `anonymise_email_triage_items(p_user_id)` (identity unlinking — NULLs
   `user_id` + `sender` under `app.email_triage_anonymise_in_progress`), and
   the scheduled retention sweep `purge_email_triage_items()` (age-based
   deletion: probe 7d, non-statutory 365d). No per-request targeted-delete
   RPC exists in v1 — the purge function is parameterless and sweeps by age
   only. If a verified erasure request requires immediate row deletion beyond
   anonymisation, it is an operator-run SQL action under the purge GUC
   (`app.email_triage_purge_in_progress`), documented per-request. No bypass
   exists outside these GUC-gated paths — the gate that makes the ledger
   trustworthy is the same gate that makes erasure auditable.
3. **Art. 17(3)(b) accountability-period override for statutory rows:** rows
   with `statutory_class` set (DSAR receipt, breach notice, service of
   process, regulator contact) are themselves the controller's evidence of
   compliance with legal obligations (Art. 12(3) response discipline, Art. 33
   notification, Art. 31 cooperation, court process). Erasing the receipt
   record of a DSAR would destroy the Art. 5(2) evidence that the DSAR was
   handled on time. These rows are therefore retained for the accountability
   period under Art. 17(3)(b) (processing necessary for compliance with a
   legal obligation) — with anonymisation of `sender` available as the
   less-intrusive partial measure where the clock evidence survives without
   the identifier. Each invocation of the override is documented per the
   statutory-response-catalog DSAR checklist.
4. **DSAR access:** `email_triage_items` is in `DSAR_TABLE_ALLOWLIST`
   (Art. 15+20, `user_id` owner) for the operator; involuntary-sender access
   requests are fulfilled manually via the service role keyed on `sender`.

### Conclusion

The controller's interests (operational + statutory, the latter directly
protective of requesting data subjects) outweigh the residual interests of
involuntary senders in not having business correspondence machine-triaged,
given: the business-context expectation of automated mail handling, the
structurally minimal persisted footprint, the bounded and disclosed
sub-processor surface, the deterministic protection of exactly the mail
classes where data subjects' own rights are at stake, and a working Art. 17
path with an honestly scoped Art. 17(3)(b) carve-out.

**Balancing outcome: legitimate interest prevails.** Art. 6(1)(f) is the
appropriate lawful basis for PA-27.
