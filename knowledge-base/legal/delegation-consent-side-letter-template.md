## How this document is used (not a paper signature)

This document is **not** a fill-in-the-blanks contract to be printed and
signed. It is the **canonical, versioned consent text** that an in-app
acceptance binds to. Earlier revisions of this template (template version
1.x) used blank Grantor/Grantee signature lines; that paper-signature
precondition is **RETIRED** and replaced by recorded in-app consent.

Both Parties are bound to **the same version** of this text:

- The **Grantee (Co-Member)** is bound when they click "I accept" in the
  application. The acceptance is recorded server-side in
  `byok_delegation_acceptances`, capturing `side_letter_version`,
  `accepted_at`, `ip_hash`, and `user_agent`. That recorded row is the
  evidence that consent was demonstrably given (Article 7(1) GDPR).
- The **Grantor (Workspace Owner)** is bound to the **same versioned text**
  at the moment of delegation creation. Neither Party can be bound to a
  different revision: both the delegation and the acceptance reference one
  identical, server-pinned `side_letter_version`.

### Version pinning and fail-closed behaviour

The version string that the running system stores in `side_letter_version`
is governed by a single server-owned constant — the TypeScript constant
`BYOK_SIDE_LETTER_VERSION` and the SQL function
`current_byok_side_letter_version()`. **The version the system currently
pins is `1.0.0`.**

This is intentionally distinct from this document's own front-matter
`template_version` (`2.0.0`), which tracks editorial revisions of the legal
text. The two are related but not the same field: the system pin
(`1.0.0`) is the value bound into each acceptance and delegation row, while
`template_version` versions the document itself. When the CLO determines a
material change to the consent text warrants re-consent, the server constant
is bumped; bumping it **fails closed** at the lease gate — acceptances
carrying a stale `side_letter_version` no longer satisfy the gate, so the
Grantee must re-accept the current version before further delegated runs are
authorised.

## Parties

This arrangement allocates responsibilities between two roles. Identities
are not transcribed onto this document; they are resolved at runtime from
the workspace membership records and the `byok_delegations` row.

- **Workspace Owner ("Grantor"):** the account holder who registered the
  BYOK API key and created the delegation.
- **Co-Member ("Grantee"):** the workspace member whose AI agent runs are
  funded under the Grantor's key, and who records consent in-app.

## 1. Scope of Delegation

1.1 The Grantor grants the Grantee access to the Grantor's API key for the purpose of running AI agent sessions within the shared workspace, subject to the spending caps set by the Grantor.

1.2 The delegation is revocable at any time by either Party. The Grantor may revoke by disabling the delegation in the team settings. The Grantee may withdraw consent at any time (see Section 6).

1.3 The Grantor sets a daily spending cap (in USD) and an optional hourly cap. The Platform enforces these caps automatically.

---

## 2. Lawful Basis (Article 6 GDPR)

This arrangement rests on two **distinct** lawful bases, which must not be conflated:

2.1 **Grantee's consent — Article 6(1)(a).** The Grantee's consent is the lawful basis for processing the Grantee's own prompt content under the Grantor's API key, and for the Grantor receiving itemised cost telemetry about the Grantee's funded runs (Section 4). This consent is freely given, specific, and informed; it is recorded in-app per Section 5 and may be withdrawn per Section 6.

2.2 **Funding relationship — Article 6(1)(b).** The `byok_delegations` row that establishes the funding relationship between Grantor and Grantee (the grant of key access, spending caps, and cost allocation) rests on Article 6(1)(b) — processing necessary for the performance of the arrangement between the Parties. This contractual-necessity basis is separate from, and does not substitute for, the Grantee's Article 6(1)(a) consent in Section 2.1.

2.3 Consent and contractual necessity are not interchangeable: withdrawing the Article 6(1)(a) consent stops the consent-based processing of the Grantee's content and telemetry, while the lawfulness of the underlying funding-relationship records continues to rest on Article 6(1)(b) for the duration and retention permitted by law.

---

## 3. Joint Controllership Arrangement (Article 26 GDPR)

3.1 For the Grantee's prompt content routed through the Grantor's API key, the Grantor and Grantee are **joint controllers** within the meaning of Article 26 GDPR. The responsibility allocation in this Section IS the Article 26 arrangement; a unilateral click does not create one — the bilateral allocation of duties below does.

3.2 Anthropic PBC remains the processor under the Grantor's existing Data Processing Agreement. No new sub-processor relationship is created by the delegation.

3.3 **Allocation of responsibilities (essence of the arrangement, made available to data subjects per Art. 26(2)):**

| Responsibility | Allocated to |
| --- | --- |
| **Data-subject access requests (DSARs)** and other data-subject rights requests (access, rectification, erasure, portability, objection) | The **Grantor** is the primary point of first contact and is responsible for receiving, logging, and coordinating responses to DSARs concerning processing under the delegation. The **Grantee** must cooperate promptly where a request concerns content the Grantee submitted. |
| **Security of processing (Art. 32)** | The **Grantor** is responsible for the security and confidentiality of the BYOK API key, for spending-cap configuration, and for maintaining the Anthropic DPA. The **Grantee** is responsible for the security of their own credentials and for not submitting content they are not authorised to process. |
| **Transparency obligations (Arts. 13–14)** | The **Grantor** is responsible for informing affected data subjects that their data may be processed under the delegated key and for making the essence of this arrangement available. The **Grantee** is responsible for the lawfulness and accuracy of the content they submit and for the transparency owed to any third-party data subjects whose data the Grantee includes in prompts. |
| **Contact point for data subjects (Art. 26(1))** | The **Grantor's** workspace-owner contact (the email on the Grantor's account) is the single contact point through which data subjects may exercise their rights. Irrespective of this allocation, the Grantee retains the right (Art. 26(3)) to exercise rights against, and a data subject may contact, either joint controller. |

3.4 Notwithstanding the allocation in Section 3.3, data subjects may exercise their GDPR rights in respect of and against each of the joint controllers (Article 26(3)).

## 4. Cost Telemetry Consent

4.1 The Grantee consents (Article 6(1)(a)) to the Grantor receiving **itemised cost telemetry** for each AI agent run funded by the delegation. This telemetry includes:
  - Token count (input and output)
  - Cost in USD cents
  - Timestamp of invocation
  - Agent role (domain leader identifier)

4.2 The Grantor does **NOT** receive access to:
  - The Grantee's prompt content
  - The AI assistant's response content
  - Any conversation history or context
  - Any personal data beyond what is listed in Section 4.1

---

## 5. Recorded In-App Consent (Article 7 GDPR)

5.1 The Grantee provides consent by an affirmative in-app action ("I accept"). There is no paper signature. The acceptance is stored server-side in `byok_delegation_acceptances`, which records:
  - `side_letter_version` — the server-pinned consent version (currently `1.0.0`) that the acceptance binds to;
  - `accepted_at` — the timestamp of the affirmative action;
  - `ip_hash` — a hashed source address;
  - `user_agent` — the client string at time of acceptance.

5.2 This recorded acceptance row is the controller's demonstration that consent was given (Article 7(1)). It supersedes the retired paper-signature lines of template version 1.x.

5.3 The Grantor is bound to the **same** `side_letter_version` at delegation creation (Section "How this document is used"), so both Parties' obligations attach to one identical revision of this text.

---

## 6. Withdrawal of Consent (Article 7(3) GDPR)

6.1 The Grantee may withdraw consent at any time. **Withdrawal is as easy to perform as giving consent** (Article 7(3)): a single in-app action, mirroring the "I accept" affordance.

6.2 Withdrawal is recorded server-side in `byok_delegation_withdrawals` (migration 084). Upon withdrawal, the consent-based processing in Sections 2.1 and 4 ceases; the next delegated run fails closed at the lease gate.

6.3 Withdrawal does not affect the lawfulness of processing carried out before withdrawal, nor the records whose lawful basis is Article 6(1)(b) (Section 2.2), which are retained per Section 7.

---

## 7. Retention and Erasure

7.1 Delegation history (grant, revoke, cap changes) is retained for 7 years per financial audit requirements.

7.2 The consent record (acceptance row) and the withdrawal record are retained for 7 years.

7.3 On account erasure (Article 17 GDPR), delegation, acceptance, and withdrawal records are anonymised via the Platform's Art. 17 cascade. The Grantor's identity is removed; the Grantee's identity is removed. Audit-trail row counts are preserved.

---

## 8. Termination

8.1 Either Party may terminate this delegation at any time without cause.

8.2 Termination by the Grantor takes effect immediately (the Grantee's next agent run will fail with a "delegation revoked" error, with a 60-second grace window for in-flight sessions).

8.3 Termination by the Grantee is effected by withdrawal of consent (Section 6). The delegation row remains but is inactive.
