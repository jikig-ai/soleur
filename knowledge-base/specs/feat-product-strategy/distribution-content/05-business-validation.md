## Discord

A technical founder building a product they use daily has a specific blind spot: they cannot tell the difference between "I need this" and "the market needs this." Soleur had 280+ merged PRs, 65+ agents across 8 domains, and daily dogfooding -- but zero external users.

So we ran a structured business validation workshop using a business-validator agent with a 6-gate framework:

1. Problem (PASS) -- twofold framing validated as real and structural
2. Customer (CONDITIONAL PASS) -- specific profile defined, but named contacts below the 5-person threshold
3. Competitive Landscape (PASS, later downgraded to CONDITIONAL PASS after competitive intel scan)
4. Demand Evidence (FLAG with OVERRIDE) -- kill criterion triggered. Only 1-2 informal conversations vs. the 5+ threshold.
5. Business Model (CONDITIONAL PASS) -- four revenue models evaluated
6. Minimum Viable Scope (PASS) -- breadth validated as minimum scope

Final verdict: PIVOT. Not from the product, but from building features to validating the thesis with real users. A 7-step action plan defined.

The kill criterion at Gate 4 is the most important finding in the entire knowledge base. It prevents building in a vacuum. The AI system told the founder: stop building, start validating.

A startup strategy consultant charges $4,000-16,000 for this scope. An accelerator values equivalent advisory at $10,000-25,000.

## IndieHackers

**Title:** My AI system told me to stop building and start validating. It was right.

**Body:**

I'm a solo technical founder. I've been building Soleur -- a Company-as-a-Service platform -- for months. 280+ merged PRs, 65+ agents across 8 departments, daily dogfooding across every function from legal to marketing to competitive intelligence.

Zero external users.

I knew this was a problem, but I kept building because the product worked -- for me. The classic founder blind spot: confusing "I need this" with "the market needs this."

So I did something unusual. I built a business validation agent and ran a structured 6-gate workshop against my own project.

**The 6 gates:**

1. **Problem** (PASS): Is the pain real, structural, and articulable without referencing a solution? Yes -- solo founders face both a capacity gap (one person, every department) and an expertise gap (one skill set, all domains required).

2. **Customer** (CONDITIONAL PASS): Who specifically? Technical solo founders across all stages. But I could only name 1-2 specific people who fit the profile. The framework requires 5+.

3. **Competitive Landscape** (CONDITIONAL PASS): 19 competitors mapped across 6 tiers. I have structural advantages, but platform-tier threats (Anthropic, Cursor, GitHub Copilot) are building features that overlap with my core proposition.

4. **Demand Evidence** (FLAG -- KILL CRITERION): Only 1-2 informal conversations. The threshold is 5+ independent signals. I chose to override the kill with strong indirect signals (Naval Ravikant on one-person billion-dollar companies, Amodei predictions, solo founder growth statistics), but the flag stands.

5. **Business Model** (CONDITIONAL PASS): Four revenue options evaluated. Viable but unvalidated.

6. **Minimum Viable Scope** (PASS): Breadth is the minimum scope because the value proposition is a full AI organization, not a single-department assistant.

**The verdict: PIVOT.**

Not away from the product. Away from building features and toward validating the thesis with real users. A 7-step action plan: publish case studies, interview 10 solo founders, test willingness to pay, define success criteria.

**The meta-story:**

The kill criterion at Gate 4 is the most important finding in my entire knowledge base. It prevents me from building in a vacuum. My own AI system looked at the evidence, applied a framework, and told me to change direction. No ego, no sunk cost bias, no "but we've come so far."

A startup strategy consultant charges $4,000-16,000 for this kind of structured validation. Accelerators value equivalent advisory at $10,000-25,000. But the price isn't the point. The point is that I now have a persistent validation function that updates when new evidence arrives -- competitive data, user conversations, demand signals -- and re-derives the verdict.

The system is honest in a way that founders, including me, are not always honest with themselves.

Full case study: https://soleur.ai/blog/case-study-business-validation/

Has anyone else used a structured framework to validate (or kill) their own product idea? I'd genuinely love to hear what frameworks worked.

## Reddit

**Subreddit:** r/solopreneur
**Title:** I ran a structured validation framework against my own product. The verdict was "stop building, start validating."

**Body:**

I'm a solo technical founder, months into building a product I use daily. 280+ pull requests merged, 65+ AI agents built, 8 business departments running. The product works great -- for me.

The problem: zero external users validating the core thesis. I was building in a vacuum and I knew it but kept shipping features because momentum felt like progress.

So I built a structured validation framework with 6 gates: problem definition, customer profiling, competitive landscape, demand evidence, business model, and minimum viable scope. Each gate produces a PASS, CONDITIONAL PASS, FLAG, or FAIL. A FLAG at the demand evidence gate triggers a kill criterion.

**What happened:**

Gates 1 and 6 passed cleanly. The problem is real (solo founders face both a capacity gap and an expertise gap) and the minimum scope is validated. But Gate 2 was conditional -- I could only name 1-2 specific people who fit my target customer profile. The framework requires 5+.

Gate 4 triggered the kill criterion. Demand evidence was thin: 1-2 informal conversations vs. a 5+ threshold. I overrode the kill with indirect signals (market trends, thought leader predictions, growth statistics), but the flag stands as a permanent record.

**The verdict: PIVOT.** Not away from the product, but away from feature development and toward user validation. Stop building, start talking to people.

The most valuable thing about this process was its honesty. As founders, we're wired to rationalize. "The product is good, users will come." A structured framework with explicit kill criteria doesn't let you do that. It surfaces the evidence gap and makes you confront it.

My 7-step plan coming out of this: publish case studies showing what the product actually does, interview 10 solo founders, test willingness to pay, define success criteria before building more.

Anyone else used a structured validation approach to test their own assumptions? Curious what frameworks other founders have found useful -- especially when you're both the builder and the judge.
